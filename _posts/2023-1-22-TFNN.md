## Introduction

Physical based neural networks such as photonic neural networks are gaining increasing interest for its potential of drastic improvement over traditional electronic-based hardware[^PhotonicCrystalNN]. As one of the simplest schemes of photonic neural network, 1-dimensional photonic crystals, or in other words, multilayer thin films are investigated in our project.

Thin films have been in practical use for a long time but the design of them is challenging because of the underlying complexity of countless interacting interference and nonlinear dispersion. There have been various methods of designing including those based on traditional gradient descent, the needle optimization method, those based on optimization techniques capable of escaping from local minimums such as simulated annealing method and those based on machine learning like reinforcement learning, etc.

In our project an analogy was made between the structure of thin films and artificial neural networks. Thus the back-propagation algorithm can be applied to get more accurate gradient information with a higher efficiency[^TFNN]. Given the large parameter space in our inverse problem, the needle method is integrated to reduce the number of hyperparameters and initial parameters required in prior to the design of our algorithm.

## Method

### Problem
The inverse design of a multi-layer thin film can be thought as a fitting problem.

- Data points to fit
  - $\vec{R_t}(\vec{\lambda_t}), \vec{T_t}(\vec{\lambda_t})$
    - where $R_t$ being the reflectance, $T_t$ being the transmittance and $\lambda_t$ being the wavelength points at which $R_t$ and $T_t$ are measured.
- Parameters adjustable to fit: the structure of the multi-layer thin film
  - the number of total layers, $n$
  - thickness of each layer, $\vec{d}$
  - *material of each layer, $\vec{m}$* (Not discussed here for simplicity)
- Fitted curve
  - Spectrums $\vec{R_d}(\lambda)$, $\vec{T_d}(\lambda)$, generated by the designed multi-layer structure

### Transfer matrix
The EM wave in thin films can be represented with two-dimensional vector function of the position inside of the film, 

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
    E^{+}(x)\\
    E^{-}(x)
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

The propagation in one homogeneous layer is then a linear transform of the vector. A diagonal matrix is enough to represent the phase shift in the layer:

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
    E^{+}(x+d)\\
    E^{-}(x+d)
\end{pmatrix}
= 
\begin{pmatrix}
    e^{-{i n(x, \lambda)d\cos{\theta(x)}\over \lambda}} & 0 \\ 
    0 & e^{i n(x, \lambda)d\cos{\theta(x)}\over \lambda}
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
    E^{+}(x)\\
    E^{-}(x)
\end{pmatrix}
,
$$

and the reflection at interfaces between two layers can be

$$
\begin{aligned}
&
\begin{pmatrix}
    n(x-\epsilon, \lambda) & n(x-\epsilon, \lambda)\\
    \cos{\theta(x-\epsilon, \lambda)} & -\cos{\theta(x-\epsilon, \lambda)}
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
        E^+(x-\epsilon)\\
        E^-(x-\epsilon)
\end{pmatrix}
\\=&
\begin{pmatrix}
    n(x+\epsilon, \lambda) & n(x+\epsilon, \lambda)\\
    \cos{\theta(x+\epsilon, \lambda)} & -\cos{\theta(x+\epsilon, \lambda)}
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
        E^+(x+\epsilon)\\
        E^-(x+\epsilon)
\end{pmatrix},
\end{aligned}
$$

for p-polarized wave and 

$$
\begin{aligned}
&
\begin{pmatrix}
    1 & 1\\
    n(x-\epsilon, \lambda)\cos{\theta(x-\epsilon, \lambda)} & -n(x-\epsilon, \lambda)\cos{\theta(x-\epsilon, \lambda)}
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
        E^+(x-\epsilon)\\
        E^-(x-\epsilon)
\end{pmatrix}
\\
=&
\begin{pmatrix}
    1 & 1\\
    n(x+\epsilon, \lambda)\cos{\theta(x+\epsilon, \lambda)} & -n(x+\epsilon, \lambda)\cos{\theta(x+\epsilon, \lambda)}
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
        E^+(x+\epsilon)\\
        E^-(x+\epsilon)
\end{pmatrix}
,
\end{aligned}

$$

for s-polarized wave. $n(x)$ stands for the refractive index at $x$ which also depends on the wavelength. $\cos{\theta{x, \lambda}}$ is the orientation of the wave vector which can be determined by Fresnel's equation. 

### Comparison to NN
The inverse design problem here is: to find a model that generates layer structure $\vec{d}, \vec{materials}$ from spectrum $f(\lambda)$


However, training a Forward Neural Network for this model is not easy. This requires the NN to learn the *physical laws* modeling the complex interference introduced by the multi-layer structure. To exploit the physical information we have already known, TFNN is proposed[^TFNN].

In the scheme of TFNN, the resemblance of transfer matrix to NNs are noticed and a back propagation algorithm is developed to
- fit the spectrum generated by TFNN to target spectrum

and then by
- map the TFNN to real layer structure 

the inverse design problem is finished.

The above scheme is based on gradient descent and thus its performance is guaranteed only in *measurements*, i.e. the cases where an initial configuration of the thin film is specified. In manufacturing, the product will be close to the desired structure in the parametric space if no mistakes occur. However, if we are to *design* a film, the initial value can only be guessed and far from the optimal solution, which gives rise to a poor performance.

### Flexible TFNN

To cope with this difficulty above, we propose an flexible TFNN design scheme, where *layer* becomes a learnable parameter.

After gradient descent reaches the local minimum, a new layer is inserted. The procedure to determine the insertion position is inspired by the needle optimization method[^needle]. We search for the position where the insertion gradient is the minimum and insert the new layer there. This guarantees that the loss function will not increase after new layers are added. 

On the other hand, whenever a layer's thickness decreases to zero, this layer is removed. 



## Result
### Optical thickness

As is investigated by Tikhonravov et al.[^otNeedle], the optical thickness of a thin film is a significant parameter in terms of its performance. However in addition to the positive correlation between optical thickness and performance that has been reported, we identified a sharp decrease in the loss function when initial optical thickness is close to the optical thickness of the layer that generated the target spectrum. See figure below.
![](/images/TFNN_OT0to2.png)

Additionally, the generalization ability of a TFNN can be well predicted by the OT. We evaluated the performance of a TFNN trained at 60 degree incidence angle at 57 or 63 deg. When OT ratio is greater than 1, the performance on the test set can not be improved by the increase in the hyperparameter of initial OT.

### Substitution

Furthermore, we examined the substitution scheme proposed in [^otNeedle] and found that the equal OT substitution is inadequate for an unchanged loss function even in the limit of very thin layers. We proposed another substitution scheme which performs better than the equal OT substitution. Moreover, it enables our TFNN training to iteratively continue and increase the robustness against local minimums.
